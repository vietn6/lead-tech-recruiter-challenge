Section One

Hiring Strategy

The order you hire defines the team's trajectory. Here's how I'd sequence the build-out — and the reasoning behind each decision.

TL;DR
  • Data Engineer first — you can't build on a foundation that doesn't exist yet
  • Data Scientist second — proprietary insights are the product differentiator, not just the model
  • ML Engineer third — productionize what your DS team proves works
  • Each role unblocks the next; hiring out of sequence wastes runway
  • Senior talent at this stage needs mission alignment, not just a job offer
1

Data Engineer

Data infrastructure is the prerequisite for everything else. Right now, AirOps has one data scientist operating without a purpose-built data layer beneath them. Pipelines are likely ad hoc, models retrain on stale exports, and the agent behaviors your product depends on can't be reliably personalized at scale. A Data Engineer builds the foundation — ingestion, transformation, storage, and data quality — that every downstream function depends on. Without this hire, your Data Scientist is spending 60% of their time on data wrangling. That's the wrong use of the role.

Critical Path
2

Data Scientist

With reliable pipelines in place, the Data Scientist can do what the role is actually for: extract proprietary signal from AirOps' unique data. This is where the moat gets built. Your agents become smarter than competitors' not because you're using a better base model — everyone has GPT-4 — but because your ranking logic, recommendation engine, and behavioral models are trained on data nobody else has. The DS hire should be someone who understands both statistical modeling and the product well enough to define which problems are worth solving first.

High Impact
3

ML Engineer

ML Engineers productionize what Data Scientists prove works. Hiring them before you have validated models to ship is premature optimization — and an expensive one. Once your DS team has prototypes that demonstrably improve user outcomes, the ML Engineer operationalizes them: model serving, feature stores, evaluation pipelines, A/B testing infrastructure, and latency optimization. This hire turns research into shipped product and enables the DS function to move faster by removing production burden from their plate.

Scale
🔍

How I assessed AirOps' needs

One data scientist + a data-as-moat thesis + a consumer-facing data product = a team that's underpowered relative to ambition. The gap isn't talent quality — it's infrastructure and capacity.

🎯

Gaps I'm solving for

Data reliability (DE), proprietary intelligence (DS), and production ML velocity (MLE). Each gap compounds the others if left unfilled.

🔗

Role interdependencies

DE pipelines unlock DS quality. DS validated models justify MLE infra. Hiring MLE before DE means building a production system on shaky ground.

AI Use & Methodology

How I used AI in this exercise

The challenge asks candidates to be transparent about AI use. Here's exactly what I did — and where I disagreed with it.

Step 1

Research & Synthesis

Used Claude to pull AirOps brand guidelines, synthesize the product positioning, and map the technical landscape of data roles at AI-native companies. This saved research time but required my own judgment on which signals mattered.

Step 2

Candidate Profile Framework

AI helped generate the evaluation criteria for each function. I pushed back on the initial framing — the first output over-indexed on credentials. I redirected toward stage-fit and mission signal, which are more predictive at this company size.

Step 3

Outreach Drafting

Used AI to draft first passes of each message, then rewrote significant portions. The AI versions were structurally sound but too polished — senior technical candidates can smell a template. I introduced deliberate specificity and one genuine question per note.

Step 4

Design & Presentation

Built this page using Claude to translate the challenge brief into an on-brand one-pager. The layout reflects AirOps' own design sensibility: dense information, scannable structure, no decorative noise. Form serves function.

Where I disagreed with AI output: The first draft of the hiring sequence had Data Science before Data Engineering — a common recommendation that prioritizes insight generation over infrastructure. At AirOps' current scale (1 DS), that sequencing produces a second DS who hits the same infrastructure ceiling as the first. The right call is the boring one: build the foundation before adding floor space.
